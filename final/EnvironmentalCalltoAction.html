Poaching

What is poaching?

Poaching is the illegal hunting, killing, or capturing of animals. People usually poach to produce products from the remains, such as: clothes, jewwlry, fabric etc. Poaching usually occurs within wildllife reserves, in which poachers illegally trespass, kill and escape; leaving the remains for the workers to dispose of.

Animals that are most vulnearable are those bearing ivory. Ivory can be used to produce pianos, jewelry, electrical appliances etc. A single pound of ivory can cost up to $1,500 and one tusk can weigh up to 250 pounds. Now, you're probably wondering, where do you get ivory from? Although rhinos are often poached for their horns, elephants carry the most ivory. One fully fully grown elephant can hold up to $750,000 of merchandise. Their tusks and teeth are immensely "valuable" on the black market. But who is carrying out these horrendous acts? Let's explore that...



Who are poachers? How do we stop them?

Poachers come from all over. Most commonly, poachers are disguised as tourists visiting a foreign country. China has the worlds largest illegal ivory market, but it has not been illegal for much time. Elephants mostly reside in Kenya, Rwanda, Uganda and Tanzania, and suprisingly enough, not all Poachers are foreigners. In 2016, the Kenyan National Park was amidst a scandal in which over 4 elephants were found poached and dead on the park grounds. It was proven that some of the Kenyan park rangers had corroborated with Chinese dealers, and helped them carry this out, in exchange for payment. So, how do we stop this from occuring? By sending a message. In April of 2016, Kenyan national parks safely removed the tusks of thousands of elephants, in order to protect them from further poaching. Instead of selling the remaining tusks, they sent the world a message. Led by then-President Uhuru Kenyatta, Kenya burnt over 100 tons of ivory. This changed global policy and the trade of ivory became illegal under the "Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species" It was only on December 31st, 2017 that the Chinese govenment made the sale of ivory illegal. This came after immense international pressure from other nations. This goes to show how change can be peacefully enforced. Although this does not necessarily mean that poaching doesn not occur, April 29th 2016 was a great day for East Africa and the protection of elephants in that region.

How can you help?

  1. Stop purchasing ivory products! - Your elephant horn necklace isn't cute! It's detrimental to the elephant population and helps black market sellers thrive.
  2. Donate to East African sanctuaries! - Even an additional $5 can help buy supplies for the animals!
  3. Report illegal ivory trade! - Numerous black market business have been discovered in East Africa because of civilian reports. When traveling, keep a lookout for such activities and report them to the local police units.
